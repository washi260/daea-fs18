Semna South (Nubia)

1965-1920BC

Sheena Washington

Sheena Surveys Semna South

Semna South is a fortress located in a region between the second and third cataracts of the Nile river in Nubia. The fortress was built during the 12th Dynasty of ancient Egypt and it was used to control the flow of valuables entering and exiting Lower Nubia and Egypt. Previous excavations that have occurred at South Semna were completed by the Sudan Antiquities Service in 1956 and 1957, and led by Jean Vercoutter, in addition to the Oriental Institute Expedition from 1966 to 1968, led by Louis V. Zabkar. Significant findings from South Semna include both archaeological artifacts and features and human remains detailed below.


Glimpse of General Geography and Geology

Among a collection of fortresses grouped around the Second Cataract stood Semna South during the 12th Dynasty, Middle Kingdom. The fortress is situated along the Nile River between current Sudan’s northern cities, Wadi Halfa and Abri, and lies in a region where Lower and Upper Nubia meet, a region known as the Batn -El-Hajar. As indicated by its name, Batn -El-Hajar, or “Belly of the Rock”, is “characterized by ‘bare granite ridges and gullies’, a narrowed Nile run, and heavy deposits of wind-blown sand" Toggle popover The fortress lies beneath a layers and beds of rock known as the Basement Complex. The Basement Complex is a geological formation of stratified rocks, mainly sedimentary and igneous rock that date Precambrian. The geology of Semna South describes an inhospitable region for Nubians, and an unsuitable region for agriculture in spite of the small deposits of fertile alluvial soil. Toggle popover
